Before diving into the data, it's evident from the design of the CFPS itself that there's an apparent inclination towards stratifying the economic study of families across various provinces and cities in China.

Over the last two decades, every province and city in China has demonstrated unique income trajectories. The China Family Panel Studies (CFPS) meticulously categorized these into large provinces like Shanghai, Liaoning, Henan, Gansu, and Guangdong, and smaller counterparts based on criteria like local GDP per capita, which served as a key index for socioeconomic status within administrative units. This survey approach reveals stark contrasts: while 1,600 households per large province were surveyed, only 400 were approached in each of the remaining smaller provinces, underscoring a significant disparity in data granularity.

Yet amidst this array of layered urban landscapes, we've identified a province that, until recent years, remained largely unnoticed. In 2017, it surged into the limelight, only to experience a precipitous fall thereafter‚ÄîHebei.

Despite not being categorized among the largest provinces in the CFPS survey, Hebei holds considerable strategic importance within the Jing-Jin-Ji (Beijing-Tianjin-Hebei) region. The province faces substantial economic development challenges, notably in achieving environmental targets and fostering a green economy.

In the geographical chessboard of Northern China, Hebei stands as a crucial piece, enveloped by Beijing on three sides and lying on the vast North China Plain. Together with Beijing and Tianjin, it forms an equilateral triangle at the heart of a thriving plain, benefitting from the convenience of modern transit routes.

Major transit routes like the Da-Guang Expressway, Jing-Jiu Railway, and Bao-Cang Expressway weave a network that connects cities such as Shijiazhuang and Baoding within a half-hour's commute. With the new Beijing Airport approximately 55 kilometers away, Hebei is well-positioned to satisfy the developmental needs of high-end, high-tech industries. It is also home to the North China Plain's largest freshwater lake, Baiyangdian, and is graced by the convergence of rivers like the Cao River and the South Reject Horse River, adding to its ecological appeal.

By all accounts, Hebei's geographical positioning seems to be a promising platform from which it could leap into the ranks of first-tier cities. However, the CFPS does not recognize it as a major city. Moreover, after taking office in 2013, President Xi Jinping proclaimed Hebei a "Metropolitan Auxiliary Node City" in response to a report on the coordinated development of the Jing-Jin-Ji region.

The Hebei Provincial New Urbanization Plan, released on March 26, 2014, clarified Baoding's role in alleviating Beijing's capital functions, translating to the relocation of administrative institutions, corporate headquarters, financial entities, universities, and research institutes from Beijing to Hebei prefecture-level city Baoding. This narrative, when woven into Hebei's fabric, always seems to return to one central theme: supporting and complementing Beijing's development. For a province ranked only 23rd in national GDP, there appears to be a dissonance between its economic status and the potential its geographical position suggests. This seeming paradox is further endorsed by the design and outcomes of the CFPS survey. In 2020, Hebei's actual household survey participation hit 728, surpassing the initial allocation of 400 and nearing Shanghai's numbers, with an oversample rate of 1.8 in 2020 and 1.57 for the past decades. This suggests an untold story about millions of lives in a land often cast as a mere 'supporting actor,' locked into an ambiguous position. As we narrow our lens to the intimate scale of domestic life, we encounter three households, each a testament to the diversity and challenge of economic sustenance in the region.

Based on the most recent data model, we filtered 728 surveyed households in Hebei Province, focusing on two key financial indicators: family asset income and non-housing debt. These two metrics directly impact household financial status and are closely related to Hebei's per capita disposable income. According to the 2020 data, the average family income for these 728 households is 60,847 yuan, with an average non-housing debt of 14,350 yuan. Subtracting the latter from the former, we derived an average disposable income of 46,497 yuan for the sampled households in Hebei. Based on this data, we selected three representative households. These households' disposable income levels fall into the 20,000 yuan range, the 50,000 yuan range, and the 70,000 yuan range, respectively. Following the principle of normal distribution, these income levels are evenly distributed around the mean income of 60,874 yuan. Therefore, we believe that selecting these three households can better represent the diversity of family incomes in Hebei Province, thus allowing for a more comprehensive exploration of the region's economic trends and current status.

In selecting these three households, we considered the following reasons: Representativeness: The income levels of these three households cover different segments of family incomes in Hebei Province, reflecting the economic diversity and complexity of the region. Data credibility: The data from these three households is highly reliable, providing valuable insights that accurately reflect the economic conditions in Hebei Province. Comparability: By comparing the situations of these three households, we can better understand the living conditions and economic pressures of families at different income levels, thus informing future research and policy-making.

After scrutinizing the circumstances of the three families, our assessment of the economic landscape in Hebei may not remain entirely sanguine. The first family, emblematic of the low-income demographic, exhibits commendable prudence in budgeting, prioritizing education expenditures despite their modest means. However, it is questionable whether such a substantial investment in education always yields commensurate returns. China's hefty per capita investment in education raises doubts about the correlation between education and wealth accumulation, especially when juxtaposed with the second family's financial struggles, where high living costs erode the lion's share of their income, and leaving little room for savings. This prompts a reevaluation of the traditional Chinese belief that knowledge inevitably leads to prosperity.

So, where does the future truly lie? This question weighs heavily on the minds of the younger generation. Yet, the traditional pathways to prosperity, anchored in "non-fixed" assets, prove inadequate in navigating these turbulent times.
